Seeing with Your Touch

What started as an individual project for the Arch 1101 Design I studio became a jumping point for further exploration into the realm of sensory substitution.

The Starting Point

I began my research with the star-nosed mole, a mammal renowned for its incredible sense of touch. I was interested in how the star-nosed mole, nearly blind, sees its world through touch, creating a mental sonogram of its environment as it brushes its hyper sensitive nose against the ground. How would a world constructed by touch information be visualized, and how would it differ from a world following the conventions of sight?

My study models and drawings explored the anatomy and function of the star-nosed mole.

The Synthesis

I became fascinated with the language in which to translate touch information, and the distortions to reality that this translation allows. How can the end condition vary depending on both input and environmental stimuli? I don't want to create a 1:1 representation of the environment. Rather, I want to abstract the touch information into something legible, but not in the conventional sense.

The Augmentation Device

My first augmentation device substitutes the star-nosed mole’s high sensitivity of touch to the human equivalent: sight. Additionally, the device allows the user to extend their sense of touch beyond their physical limits— the steel wires are three feet long.

The Final Machine

The final machine is similar to my previous augmentation device, with two main differences: an all-steel construction allowing for higher precision, and an archival aspect recording the interacted environment. I conducted a series of experiments to figure out the most effective manner in which to transfer ink onto paper. I decided on rubber "feet" affixed to flexible brass strips that change shape depending on pressure applied to the steel rods.

The machine allows the user to “see” beyond the scope of their physical and sensory capabilities. Each of the twenty-four rods are designed to transfer ink onto a roll of paper when manipulated from the opposite end. A small electric motor feeds the roll of paper through the machine at a slow, constant rate, recording time on the Y axis. As the user walks around the environment, the rods transfer information of surface conditions to the paper, and the user can “see” the translated conditions as ink marks from behind the paper.

The topography of the environment becomes a series of irregular marks within equally spaced channels. The density, continuity, and weight of the ink changes, but they always reside within their respective channel. This false topography is both real and imagined, totally honest yet deceitful at the same time.
